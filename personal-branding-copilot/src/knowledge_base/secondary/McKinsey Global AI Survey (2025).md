# McKinsey Global AI Survey (2025) — Comprehensive Summary

## Executive Summary

**Title:** The State of AI: Global Survey 2025  
**Focus:** Enterprise-level AI adoption, value creation, workflows

### Key Trends
- **AI usage widespread:** ~88% of organizations report using AI in at least one business function, though most remain in pilot or early scaling phases
- **AI Agents emerging:** ~62% of companies experimenting with AI agents; ~23% scaling them in at least one area
- **Scaling uneven:** Only minority deeply embedded AI across workflows; adoption often limited to marketing, sales, IT, and knowledge management

### Value & Impact
- Majority report positive signal on innovation, customer satisfaction, strategic advantage
- Enterprise-wide EBIT impact remains modest (~39% reporting any EBIT effect)
- Growth-oriented performers (setting innovation goals, redesigning workflows) more likely to see measurable outcomes

### Strategic Insights
- Tools widely adopted, but strategic integration and workflow redesign are real differentiators
- Many organizations still testing and validating use cases before scaling

## Detailed Summary

The McKinsey Global AI Survey 2025 provides one of the most extensive assessments of AI adoption, value creation, organizational impact, and scaling maturity across industries worldwide, drawing on thousands of executive responses.

## 1. Widespread Adoption But Uneven Integration

AI adoption nearly ubiquitous across large enterprises and medium-sized organizations:
- Estimated 70–90% of surveyed companies report using AI in at least one business function
- Generative AI accelerated adoption since 2023, expanding use cases beyond analytics and automation

Despite high adoption rates, stark divide exists between experimentation and scaled deployment:
- Initial use cases common (automated responses, internal analytics)
- Only minority scaled AI across multiple functions with measurable impact
- Adoption often remains concentrated within isolated pilots without broader integration

## 2. Use Cases & Functional Distribution

Most prevalent functional areas:

- **Marketing & Sales:** Personalization, lead scoring, recommendation engines, content generation
- **Customer Service:** Chatbots, virtual assistants, automated triage
- **Information Technology:** IT operations, code assistance, security monitoring, optimization
- **Product Development & Innovation:** AI-assisted design, prototyping, scenario planning

Depth of integration varies—AI-supported tasks often adjunct activities rather than fully embedded workflow components

## 3. Value Creation vs. Value Capture

Noteworthy finding: difference between adoption and actual economic value captured

McKinsey estimates generative AI could contribute **$2.6–$4.4 trillion annually** to global economy—signaling enormous potential

However, at organizational level, share reporting significant bottom-line impact remains modest:
- Roughly **25–30%** describe material improvements to revenue or cost performance attributable to AI

**Attributes common among high-value capturing organizations:**
- Clear governance models
- Alignment of AI projects with strategic objectives
- Rigorous metrics and performance measurement
- Thoughtful workflow redesign rather than superficial automation

## 4. Barriers to Scaling AI

Key obstacles consistently emerge:

- **Lack of ownership and accountability:** Projects lack clear executive sponsor or cross-functional owner
- **Data infrastructure gaps:** Struggles with quality, silos, accessibility preventing reliable development
- **Talent shortages:** Ongoing shortage of skills for design, implementation, maintenance (data science, MLOps, governance)
- **Organizational resistance and culture:** Stalls due to risk aversion, lack of adoption incentives, fear of disruption

Barriers rooted in organizational process, culture, and capability—AI transformation as management challenge as much as technical

## 5. Workforce Impact and Expectations

Nuanced picture of anticipated workforce impact:
- Some job displacement likely for routine tasks, but net reduction not dominant expectation
- Many expect productivity gains and role shifts as workers freed for higher-value work
- Organizations with strong learning cultures and reskilling programs more likely to view AI as collaborative augmentation

Workforce planning and skill development becoming critical components of sustainable adoption

## 6. Organizational Capabilities and Best Practices

Common best practices among value-realizing companies:

- **Leadership alignment:** Unified vision across C-suite and operational leadership
- **Governance frameworks:** Formal oversight managing risk, compliance, ethics while enabling scale
- **Human-in-the-loop design:** Ongoing human supervision and escalation paths
- **Iterative experimentation:** Agile cycles allowing quick test, learn, iterate

These practices differentiate organizations that remain in pilot mode from those achieving transformative outcomes

## 7. Implications for Consultants and SMEs

- Adoption without strategy rarely produces economic value—tie projects to measurable objectives
- Workflow redesign precedes automation—layering AI on inefficient processes amplifies inefficiency
- Leadership sponsors and accountable owners matter—even small organizations benefit from clear ownership
- Focus on narrow, measurable use cases initially—quick wins build confidence and alignment
- Skill and capability development is competitive advantage (technical and business literacy)

## 8. Key Strategic Takeaways

1. AI adoption no longer question of if—it's about how well organizations embed into operations
2. Economic value capture lags adoption—value creation possible only with disciplined execution
3. Leadership alignment, governance, workflow redesign essential enablers of scale
4. Workforce planning, reskilling, human-centered design differentiate successful adopters
5. SMEs have opportunities to leapfrog by focusing on high-impact, narrow use cases with clear metrics
